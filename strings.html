Guitar String types

There are all sorts of different types of string's available for the Guitar and selecting a type can vary depending on what type of Guitar, sound and over all feel you would like to gain from playing the Guitar, this is because every string has good qualities and can be perfect for your unique Guitar and playing style.


Choosing the right string's for your Classical Guitar can have a noteworthy (excuse the pun) impact on your Guitar’s tone, playability and even durability.


If you have been playing for a while now, you will know which string's are right for you. If you are complete beginner, here is quick guide to get you started.


In this lesson we will learn about which string's to buy for any 6 stringed Guitar and also how to fit them. On the back of any Guitar String packet you will find instructions on what order the strings go onto your guitar. If you are complete beginner, don't worry it is relatively simple and if mistakes are made just reverse what you have done and try again, its all part of the learning process. The next lesson covers Standard Tuning.

The very first thing you need to understand is that Acoustic or electric Guitar and Classical/flamenco Guitars use completely different kinds of string's, even though these instruments are similar in appearance. The construction difference between Acoustic and Classical/flamenco Guitars is the reason why they are fitted with different kinds of string's. Classical Guitars just aren’t built tough enough to withstand the tension of steel string's. Where as Electric Guitar's have 3 wound steel string's and Acoustic Guitars have 4, Electric Guitars are built more robust and can accommodate this, however, it doesn't produce the signature tone that this type of Guitar was made and is popular for.

Classical Guitar string's

Nylon string's are used on Classical Guitars. Nylon string's produce far less tension (around 50% less) than steel string's so the construction of the Guitars differ. So, don't use Steel string's on your Classical Guitar. it will damage it.

Though any style can make use of Classical Guitars/Nylon string's, typically they are used for Classical, flamenco and folk.

On a Classical Guitar, as shown in the above photograph, there are two types of string's. The bottom 3 string's (G, B and E in standard tuning) are made of clear Nylon and the top bass string's (E, A and D) are wound in Bronze.

Acoustic Guitar string's

Acoustic Guitar string's have 2 Nickel plated steel string's for string's E and B (standard tuning) and string's E, A, D and G(the bass string's) are wound in Bronze.

There are string gauges (thicknesses) to be aware of too, which are extra light, light, medium and heavy gauges. As a beginner it would be wise to use a light gauge string as it can help with finger movements when learning. Medium and Heavy gauges are used by more experienced Guitarists who want the string's to push back on them more when playing (usually when playing Punk. Metal and Funk), which cant be done by a beginner.

Electric Guitar string's

Electric Guitar string's have 3 Nickel plated steel string's for string's E, B and G (standard tuning) and string's E, A, D and G(the bass string's) are wound in Nickel too.

The gauges throughout are standard, meaning no matter if you are buying Classical, Acoustic or Electric Guitar string's the gauge description are exactly the same. Again, Medium and Heavy gauges are used by more experienced Guitarists who want the string's to push back on them more when playing (usually when playing Punk. Metal and Funk), which cant be achieved by a beginner as the resistance is too great.

How to re-string your Guitar

In this section we will learn how to re-string any of the Guitars already mentioned. These sections are quite lengthy and if not relevant to you then feel free to scroll down until you get to your type of Guitar.

After selecting your string the next stage is to string, or if you are replacing your string's, then keep reading as this is relevant if you are restringing your instrument too.

Stringing a Classical Guitar

Stringing a Classical Guitar is different to stringing any other Guitar as the string's don't have a stopper ring at the end of the string, so you have to tie them yourself once posted through the string guide holes on the Bridge. There a few ways to do this and below I have created some handy videos.

Step 1 - The Bass string's.

The bass string's on a Classical Guitar need less tension in the knot as opposed to the three remaining Nylon string's. This is because, Nylon string's stretch a lot over time. They eventually become, "dead," and un-tunable - this is when you know when to change your string's.

Once you have threaded the string through the Bridge, you should (1) loop the string back over the Bridge and underneath the string, then (2) between the Bridge and the string form a knot. Finally, (3) pull tight.

Step 2 - The Treble (Nylon) string's.

The bass string's on a Classical Guitar need less tension in the knot as opposed to the three remaining Nylon string's. This is because, Nylon string's stretch a lot over time. They eventually become, "dead," and un-tunable - this is when you know when to change your string's.

Once you have threaded the Nylon string's through the Bridge, you should (1) loop the string back over the Bridge and underneath the string, then (2) between the Bridge and the string form a knot and (3) repeat this until 3 knots are made. Finally, loop back under the string and (4) pull tight.

Stringing Fixed Bridged Guitars

Stringing an Acoustic Guitar and some Electric Guitars with fixed Bridge's. These sort of Bridge's allow the sting to be threaded through it, allowing for string's to be replaced with the Guitar lying down with it's strings facing upwards. Below are two images of an Acoustic Bridge and Fixed Electric Guitar Bridge. There are two major types of Fixed Bridges. Fender’s hard-tail which is what you can find on a Stratocaster guitar, and a Tune-O-Matic Bridge made popular by Gibson Les Paul. There are other variations of bridge types, but most of them draw inspiration from these two.

Acoustic Guitar Bridge

Below the Sound hole of an Acoustic Guitar's you will find the Bridge. The string's are pushed into holes within the Bridge - loop end first and then held into place with pegs. These are easily identifiable in the image above as the holding peg's here have a metallic finish to them here, although they can come in wood, plastic or metal.

The Guitar string's are then fed up through designated string notches in the Saddle (white Nylon as seen the image above) , along the Fret Board and into the tuning pegs found in the Guitar's Headstock.

Fixed Electric Guitar Bridge

Fixed Electric Guitar Bridges are typically made of steel, and are housed below the Saddle and below the bottom pickup. The Bridge in the image above is a standard Bridge type, the string's are posted through the base of the Bridge (loop last) and pulled tightly. If sone correctly, the loop of the Guitar string is fixed snug in the Bridge.

The string is then fed over its designated place in the Saddle (typically made from steel as opposed to Nylon an Acoustic guitar) and fed upto the tuning pegs in the Headstock.

Stringing a Tremolo Bridged Guitar

Stringing an Electric Guitar with a Tremolo Bridge, seems more difficult but it really isn't. The guitar string's are fed in (loop last), through a specially designed plate at the back of the guitar. The order of the strings are left to right are,E, A, D, G, B and higher E. If you are complete beginner, don't worry it is relatively simple and if mistakes are made just reverse what you have done and try again, its all part of the learning process.

Once posted through these holes, it is just a case of pulling the strings along the designated grooves on the bridge (see above image) along the fretboard, though the corresponding groove on the nut (previously described in the basics lessons) and onto the head stock to the designated tuning pegs more on this in Lesson 2 - Tuning your guitar.

The fretboard is an important component on most stringed instruments. It is a thin, long strip of wood, that is laminated to the front of the neck of a string instrument. The string's run over the fingerboard, between the nut and the Bridge.
